# DHH-persona review — round 2 (fresh reviewer, verbatim, unsoftened)

1. **The golden path teaches mass assignment as lesson one.** `(db/insert! tx :users (:body req))` — the raw request body, shoveled straight into the users table. We got Rails publicly humiliated over exactly this in 2012 when a kid committed to rails/rails master via mass assignment on GitHub. It took a security incident to force strong parameters into the framework, and here you are in 2026 printing the vulnerability in the tutorial, on the line the spec calls "the product." Yes, day two has `db/cast` with a schema. Day one has an open POST endpoint that writes arbitrary columns. The blessed path IS the security posture — the golden page must cast, or `insert!` must refuse a bare request body. No third option.

2. **Your own demo ships without the CSRF protection you brag about.** The spec: sessions, CSRF, secure cookies "on by default in the scaffold." The golden path: `:middleware [(http/access-log) (http/recover)]`. That's it. No session middleware, no CSRF, and a state-mutating POST route. So either the scaffold generates a different, longer middleware vector than the page you're showing me — meaning the advertised page is fiction — or the defaults aren't defaults, they're documentation. In Rails the security middleware is there *because you didn't write anything*. In a "you call it, it never calls you" framework, every safety feature is opt-in by construction, and your flagship demo just demonstrated the failure mode. This is the structural cost of your purity slogan and you haven't paid it, you've hidden it.

3. **"It never calls you" is marketing, not architecture.** `jobs/start!` spawns goroutines that invoke your handlers forever. `http/serve` installs a SIGTERM handler and drains *your* in-flight work. The `recover` middleware catches your exceptions and decides your status codes. That's the framework calling you — you've just insisted the summons arrive through a function argument and declared victory. Fine as a taste preference! But stop selling it as a categorical difference from Rails while your spec's own escape clause — "the only inversion is an adapter invoking a handler/job fn the user passed" — describes... a framework. Rack does exactly this. You've reinvented Rack and called it a philosophy.

4. **Top-level defs doing I/O contradict your one honest sentence.** `(def cfg (config/load!))` and `(def pg (db/connect! ...))` execute at *namespace load*, not in `-main`. Require this namespace in a test and you've connected to a database. The spec even flinches: "every framework effect traces to a call in the user's `-main` **(or file top-level)**" — that parenthetical is where the whole "boot order is main" claim goes to die. You mocked lifecycle libraries out of existence and then rediscovered, in your first sample app, exactly why Clojure grew three of them. Either bless load-time effects and own the testing consequences in writing, or move construction into `-main` and admit the page gets longer. Right now you're doing the Spring thing: the slogan in the ADR, the exception in the footnote.

5. **The var-vs-fn liveness trap is a beginner-shaped hole.** `#'signup` derefs per request, live at the REPL; a plain `fn` doesn't. So the beginner — who has never seen `#'` in their life — writes `signup` without the reader macro, re-evals at the REPL, sees nothing change, and concludes the flagship feature is broken. Your headline demo depends on two characters of punctuation whose omission fails *silently*. Rails would never ship a feature where the magic evaporates if you forget a sigil. Demand: in dev mode, routes take symbols-or-fns and liveness just works; or `cljgo dev` warns loudly when a route holds a dead fn. Sharp knives cut vegetables, not the cook's first fifteen minutes.

6. **The `summary` handler is your fifteen-minute pitch and it's a five-level nested horror.** `(:text (cache/fetch mem [:summary (:id user)] #(ai/generate! (ai/model cfg :summarizer) {:prompt (str ...)})))` — inside a `:p`, inside `html/page`, inside `http/ok`, inside a `let`. Count the closing parens on line 56. This is the code you chose *when you controlled everything*, the best it will ever look, and it reads like a lisp obfuscation contest. Worse: it's a synchronous AI call inside a request handler, so the new user's first page load takes three seconds and their first production lesson is a timeout. Nobody's fifteen-minute magic includes an OpenAI bill and a cold-cache stall. Cut `keel.ai` from the golden page entirely; show me signup, a query, a job, a page. That's the app everyone actually builds.

7. **`keel.ai` as a core pillar is trend-chasing bolted to the keel.** Rails doesn't ship `rails.ai` next to ActiveRecord, and not because we didn't notice LLMs exist. Databases and HTTP have thirty-year-stable semantics; "cross-provider fallback chains" and "native JSON modes" describe this quarter's vendor churn. Baking it into the framework's blessed seven namespaces means the framework's release cadence is now coupled to Anthropic's changelog. It's T4 — last tier, first thing to rot. Make it a first-party *satellite* library with its own version number, keep the keel for things that will mean the same thing in 2036.

8. **Four config layers plus a profile plus a schema file is Spring in EDN cosplay.** Schema defaults → conf.edn → conf.profile.edn → APP_* env, with `APP_DB_HOST` → `[:db :host]` munging rules, a required `conf.schema.edn` on day one, and a `cljgo config` subcommand whose existence is a confession — you built a resolution system complicated enough to need forensic tooling, then shipped the forensic tooling and called it a feature. Rails: `config/database.yml`, ENV wins, done, no schema file, no explain command, and nobody debugs it at 2 a.m. because there's nothing to debug. Two layers: file, env. If you can't live with two, the "simplicity as the core value" line comes out of the ADR.

9. **The error funnel is the exact magic you swore off, unspecified.** "A constraint violation becomes a 422 without ceremony" — becomes? Via what registry of exception-type → status-code mappings, decided by whom, extended how? That's a framework opinion executing invisibly inside middleware — precisely the "hiding the call graph" you diagnose as Spring's disease, on the very same page. And `recover` also catches "stray `(err e)` values," meaning when a beginner confuses your two error worlds and returns a Result as a response, the framework silently launders the type error into a status code instead of teaching them. I actually *like* funnels — Rails maps RecordNotFound to 404 — but I wrote down the mapping and how to override it. Your spec has a vibe where the table should be.

10. **The `!` / Result split is two dialects sold as "one rule."** Beginners live in `db/insert!`; day two they "drop the `!`" and every call site changes meaning — same function name, bang removed, now returns a wrapped value that will happily flow into your hiccup vector as `#Result[...]` if they forget `let?`. Every third-party keel library must now ship both surfaces or feel foreign in one of the two dialects. Rails has exactly this convention (`save`/`save!`) and I'll tell you what twenty years taught us: half of all codebases use the wrong one everywhere and every code review relitigates it. You've made the schism *load-bearing* — the http layer speaks exceptions, the db layer's plain forms speak Result, and the funnel exists to mop up the confusion. One error story for the golden path, all the way down, or the "one rule language-wide" claim is false advertising.

11. **There is no static file story. At all.** I read the whole spec. `keel.html` renders markup — served how, styled how? No `http/static`, no mention of a `public/` directory in the blessed layout, no way to get one line of CSS onto the "rendered page" the fifteen minutes supposedly ends with. I am the last person to demand an asset pipeline — #NOBUILD, gladly — but "no asset pipeline" was supposed to mean *vanilla CSS served straight from disk*, not *no CSS has ever existed*. Your first page looks like a 1994 physics department homepage and your spec has no requirement that could fix it. One line: `["GET /static/" (http/dir "public")]` in the scaffold. It's missing, and the fifteen minutes is bald without it.

12. **The scaffold generates a "subset" — the bait-and-switch is in your own tasks file.** Task 0.1, in writing: `cljgo new` emits a "golden-path *subset*." Of course it does — the page uses jobs (T3), cache (T3), and AI (T4), which won't exist when T0 ships. So the actual first fifteen minutes is some undesigned, unreviewed fraction of the page, and the page you DHH-proofed three times is the one nobody gets for four tiers. The demo you polish must be the demo the generator writes, byte for byte. Design the T0 page — it's the most important file in the project and it currently doesn't exist.

13. **The honesty note admits the headline feature doesn't run.** Buried under the PASS table: the live-handler demo needed "a Go bridge embedding the real evaluator" because the interpreter can't do net/http yet. So `cljgo new && cljgo dev` — the thing at the top of the verdict, present tense, styled as a terminal transcript — currently boots nothing. I respect the disclosure; I do not respect its placement. The ADR then cites the spike as demonstrating claims "against the real runtime," which is precisely one bridge short of true. Move the caveat to the top of the verdict or stop typesetting fiction as a shell session.

14. **Stringly-typed everything at the seams.** `"POST /signup"` — method and path fused into one string, unparsed until runtime. `{:ttl "5m"}` — a duration as a string, in a Lisp, where data literals are the entire religion. `:email/welcome` as a job key, matched against a handler map at dispatch — typo the keyword at the enqueue site and the failure arrives asynchronously, later, in a worker log. And `{:id "7"}` — params bind as strings, the golden path passes `$1` straight to a query against what is presumably an integer column, so the flagship page 500s on a pgx type error unless there's coercion nobody specified. Routes-as-data was the pitch; this is routes-as-string-with-brackets-around-it.

15. **The documents grade themselves and left the answer key blank.** The verdict opens by announcing it survived "three adversarial DHH-persona review rounds" — then Round 2 is an empty heading, Round 3 is an empty heading, and "Open questions for the owner" says "(populated after round 3)". You cited reviews that aren't in the document as evidence of hardening, in the artifact whose whole job is to be the evidence. Meanwhile "guides gate releases like code" — a governance clause — sits next to a spec that shipped with placeholder sections. Process that audits everything except itself is my favorite genre of enterprise theater. Either the rounds happened and their findings go in the file, or the sentence claiming them comes out.

16. **"Replaceable in principle because it is only a library" — no it isn't.** keel ships fused to the toolchain; `cljgo new`, `cljgo dev`, `cljgo migrate`, and `cljgo config` all presuppose it; the generator writes `keel.*` requires into every file you own. That's fine — that's what a framework IS, and omakase means committing — but then the namespace-purity hedge ("keel must be replaceable... precisely because it is only a library") is a fig leaf for people who want framework benefits with library plausible-deniability. Rails never pretended you could swap it out. Pick the confidence of your convictions: you are shipping a framework. Say the word without flinching.

**Verdict.** Would this make me quit Rails? No — but it's the first Clojure attempt I've seen that fails for interesting reasons instead of boring ones. The skeleton is genuinely right: one blessed way per pillar, no ORM, no DI container, jobs in Postgres, stdlib routing, docs as release gates — someone here has actually read the Doctrine rather than just resenting it. But the golden page — the one artifact this entire process exists to perfect — teaches mass assignment, omits its own CSRF story, blocks on an AI call, leaks I/O into namespace load, and can't currently execute; the config system needs an explain command; the error model is two dialects with a middleware mop; and the fifteen-minute experience the generator will actually produce has never been designed. Rails won because the first page was magical *and honest* — what you saw in the screencast was what `rails new` gave you. Right now keel's screencast is a matte painting. Fix the page — really fix it, until a nineteen-year-old with no `#'` in their vocabulary ships something secure and styled before their coffee cools — and this becomes the framework Clojure spent twenty years too proud to build. Ship it as drafted, and it's a very good library collection with a verdict file that reviewed itself.
